Research

In 2003-2004, Prof. David Muller returned to Cornell after several years at Bell Labs. Then a second-year undergraduate, I began working for him, analyzing copper grains of electroplated copper wafers with the LEICA 440 SEM (property of Cornell Center for Materials Research). I was able to image the copper grains after ion milling. I was also able to identify different grains via differing diffraction patterns using an electron backscatter detector in the same SEM.

This year, I am again working for Prof. Muller. This time, his project for me involves the study of pentacene as an organic semiconductor. It is important for this application to characterize the grains of pentacene. Electrical resistance at grain boundaries more or less determines the resistance of the pentacene in general. So far, I have analyzed 40 nm thin films of pentacene in the SEM above, in the CCMRs FEI Tecnai G20 TEM/STEM, and in the CCMRs Veeco Dimension 3100 Scanning Probe Microscope. Very little information was obtainable from the SEM images, but some of the other images are linked below.

AFM Images of Pentacene Grains / Estimation of Grain Size

These images were taken in the Dimension 3100 AFM in Clark Hall. The sample is 40 nm of Pentacene vapor-deposited on amorphous Si3N4 on top of bulk silicon. Preliminary grain analysis gives an average grain diameter of about 1 um.

Estimation of Grain Size from a STEM Image

This image was taken in the FEI Tecnai G20 in STEM mode at 200 keV accelerating voltage. The sample is the same as above, without the underlying bulk silicon. Note the increased difficulty in determining grain boundaries. Preliminary grain analysis gives an average grain diameter of less than 1 um. The origin of this discrepancy is still under investigation. It is possibly due to differing cooling rates with and without the bulk silicon during vapor deposition.
